Some days, even simple tasks feel exhausting.

Not because they’re difficult — but because deciding to do them feels heavier than the task itself.

This experience has a name. And once you understand it, a lot of self-blame starts to dissolve.

## What Decision Fatigue Actually Is

Decision fatigue isn’t about poor choices.

It’s what happens when the brain is asked to evaluate too many things for too long without relief.

Every decision — small or large — draws from the same mental reserve:
- what to do first
- what can wait
- what matters most
- what happens if you choose wrong

When that reserve runs low, the brain doesn’t stop functioning. It shifts strategy.

It avoids.

## Why Simple Things Start Feeling Hard

Under decision fatigue, tasks don’t feel heavy because they’re complex.

They feel heavy because each one requires:
- prioritising
- estimating effort
- predicting consequences

When your system is already overloaded, those micro-decisions pile up faster than you can process them.

So the brain delays action — not out of laziness, but as a protective response.

## Why Pushing Through Backfires

A common response to decision fatigue is to apply more discipline.

“Just choose something.”  
“Stop overthinking.”

But forcing decisions when capacity is depleted increases strain. The result is often:
- rushed choices
- regret
- more avoidance later

This creates the illusion that you’re bad at decisions, when in reality you’re making too many without recovery.

## The Shift That Reduces Load

Decision fatigue eases when the number of active choices drops.

Not when you optimise harder — but when:
- the horizon narrows
- defaults replace deliberation
- fewer things demand immediate attention

Clarity returns when the system becomes quieter, not when it becomes stricter.

## Living With Fewer Decisions

When decision load decreases, people often notice:
- steadier energy
- easier starts
- less internal resistance
- fewer abandoned tasks

Nothing dramatic changed.  
The mental noise simply lowered enough for action to resume.
