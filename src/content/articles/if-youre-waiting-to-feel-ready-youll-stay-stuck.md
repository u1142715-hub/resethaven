A lot of people delay change for a reasonable-sounding reason.

They’re waiting to feel ready.

Ready means:
- more energy
- more clarity
- less chaos
- the right mood
- confidence that it will work this time

The problem is that readiness rarely arrives when life is already heavy.

## Why Readiness Doesn’t Arrive Under Pressure

When you’re overwhelmed, the nervous system aims for safety, not growth.

It reduces risk-taking, lowers tolerance for uncertainty, and prioritises short-term relief. In that state, waiting to feel ready can become a long-term holding pattern.

You don’t feel ready because your system is protecting itself.

## How Waiting Increases Pressure

Waiting to feel ready often increases the weight of the decision.

The longer you wait, the more the task represents:
- missed time
- accumulated guilt
- fear of failing again

So when you finally try to start, it feels heavier than it needed to be.

This isn’t procrastination in the lazy sense.

It’s hesitation under load.

## The Missing Shift: Start Without Readiness

Readiness is often the result of starting, not the prerequisite.

Confidence builds after small closures. Clarity follows action when the action is small enough to be survivable.

This is not about forcing yourself.

It’s about choosing a start that is smaller than the resistance.

## What Makes Starting Possible

Starting becomes possible when:
- the next step is contained
- the cost of failure is low
- the system allows recovery
- progress doesn’t require a perfect run

When those conditions exist, you don’t need to feel ready.

You can simply begin.

You’re cautious — and probably overloaded.
